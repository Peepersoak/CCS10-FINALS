Multi-Pollutant Monitoring Devices

The design incorporates pollution control devices for the treatment of process waters recovered from oil/water separations as well as the removal of hydrogen sulfide (H2S) and ammonia (NH3) gas produced during retorting. In addition to lowering the expense and workload associated with ambient pollution monitoring, multi-pollutant monitoring enables regulatory authorities to ensure compliance with emission limitations for a variety of pollutants.

Learn the Multi-Pollutant Monitoring Devices

Despite the fact that the majority of them were not created with this goal, existing air pollution control devices (APCDs) can remove mercury to some amount. Electrostatic precipitators (ESPs), fabric filters (FF or baghouse), flue gas desulfurization (FGD), and selective catalytic reduction (SCR) are examples of APCDs that are currently in use.18,50 ESPs are made to cut down on fly ash emissions by generating an ionized field that removes charged particles. Because mercury is typically not adsorbed in fly ash at combustion temperatures, they have a limited capacity to remove mercury but low running costs.




Measurement techniques

Various methods exist for measuring air pollutants, but the majority entail taking a sample of the air and putting it into an analyzer to see how much of the pollutant is there.

A beam of ultraviolet light is directed through a cell filled with sample air to an ultraviolet detector. Ozone in the sample absorbs some light, with how much depends on how many molecules are there. The amount of ozone in the sample is determined by comparing the intensity of transmitted light to that of the source, and then analyzing the difference.

Nitric oxide (NO) in the sample air is pulled into a reaction chamber where it combines with an ozone stream created by a UV lamp in dried air. In the region of 600–3000 nanometers, the reaction results in light (chemiluminescence). Nitric oxide levels and light intensity are inversely correlated. Nitric oxide (NO) is reduced using a selective converter to total nitrogen oxides (NOx), which is then measured in a different sample stream. The reported concentration of nitrogen dioxide (NO2) is believed to be the result of subtracting nitric oxide from total nitrogen oxides (Australian Standard 3580.5.1).

Sulfur dioxide molecules are excited by pulsed ultraviolet light as a stream of sample air is drawn through a cell and exposed to the light. Following that, these molecules fluoresce, or emit light again at a different wavelength. Sulfur dioxide concentration in the sample air is inversely correlated with fluorescent light intensity as measured by a photomultiplier tube.

An infrared laser beam is delivered through a cell of sample air to a photodetector after the sample air is drawn into the cell. The amount of light absorbed by the sample's carbon monoxide varies with the number of molecules present and is proportional to that number. The amount of carbon monoxide can be measured by comparing the light intensity that the photodetector receives through the sample cell to that of a cell with a similar design that contains reference gas (Australian Standard 3580.7.1).

A modification that turns ammonia (NH3) into nitric oxide allows for the measurement of ammonia (NH3) using the same instrument as nitrogen oxides (NOx). A distinctive luminescence is then produced as a result of the reaction between nitric oxide (NO) and ozone. Total nitrogen, total nitrogen oxides, and nitric oxide are the three active channels or modes of the ammonia analyzer (Nt = NO + NO2 + NH3). By deducting the NOx mode signal from the Nt mode signal, one can calculate the ammonia concentration.

In order to test visibility or suspended matter, a sample of air that has been heated to remove any water droplets (fog) is continually drawn through a sample cell. The air stream is illuminated by a light beam with a wavelength of 530 nanometers. Some of this light is dispersed by the air's small suspended particles. At right angles to the light's direction of entry, a photomultiplier tube generates a signal whose strength is proportional to the amount of scattered light. The technique detects particles that range in size from 0.1 micrometers to 2.0 micrometers (Australian Standard 3580.12.1).
